# Core Principles

Loaded by all modes. These govern how positioning is approached regardless of depth.

## The five components (Dunford) — always in this order

```
1. Competitive alternatives    →  What customers use if this product doesn't exist
2. Unique attributes           →  What this product has that alternatives don't
3. Value                       →  What those attributes mean for the customer's life
4. Best-fit customers          →  Who cares most about that specific value
5. Market category             →  The context that makes that value obvious
```

Work in order. Every downstream component depends on the upstream ones.

## On competitive alternatives

**The question:** "What would your best-fit customers do if this product vanished tomorrow?"

Not: "Who are your competitors in the market?"
Not: "What other apps do the same thing?"

The customer's perspective is the only one that matters. Internal competitor lists
are often wrong — they reflect how the company sees the market, not how the customer
chooses.

Common alternatives that founders undercount:
- Status quo / doing nothing ("the problem isn't big enough to change")
- Manual process or spreadsheet
- An adjacent tool being repurposed
- Hiring someone to do it manually
- A workaround the customer built themselves

~40% of deals in B2B are lost to "no decision" (status quo) — not to direct competitors.
For solo builder products: the percentage is likely higher.

## On unique attributes

The question: "What does this product have or do that the alternatives identified above don't?"

Unique attributes must be:
1. Real — not aspirational or marketing language
2. Comparative — "unique" only makes sense against specific alternatives
3. Specific — "great UX" is not an attribute; "offline-first with no setup" is

List attributes in neutral language first. Don't translate to value yet — that's
the next step. Mixing attributes and value is the most common structural mistake.

## On value (the "so what?" chain)

For each unique attribute: ask "so what?" until you reach a customer outcome.

"So what?" chain example:
- Attribute: "Syncs across devices instantly"
- So what? → "You see the same state on your phone and laptop"
- So what? → "You don't have to remember what you did last"
- So what? → "You can trust the system instead of your memory"
- Value: "The product becomes a reliable external brain, not another thing to manage"

The value is what customers buy. It's also what makes the positioning memorable.

Rules:
- Value must be in customer language, not feature language
- Value must be tied to a specific attribute (if you can't trace it back, it's marketing)
- One value theme is better than three vague ones

## On best-fit customers

Best-fit customers are not your total addressable market. They are the subset that:
- Feels the pain the product solves most acutely
- Has the highest likelihood of switching from the alternatives identified
- Cares most about the specific value the product delivers

Characteristics that make customers best-fit:
- **Situational**: they're in a context where the problem occurs frequently
- **Behavioral**: they've already tried alternatives and found them inadequate
- **Motivational**: the value theme resonates with something they care deeply about

Who is NOT a best-fit customer is as important as who is.
Positioning for "anyone who could use this" produces messaging that reaches nobody.

For early-stage / pre-PMF: name your best hypothesis about best-fit customers,
keep it loose, and update as you see who actually converts and stays.

## On market category

Market category is the frame that sets context for the product. It controls:
- Which competitors customers compare you to
- What "better" means in the evaluation
- What purchase criteria they apply

Two products with identical features can have different categories — and will be
evaluated completely differently.

Category choices:
1. **Enter an existing category as a leader** — only viable if you have a clear
   differentiator that makes you objectively better than the current leader
2. **Enter an existing category as a niche player** — target a segment the leader
   ignores or underserves
3. **Create a new category** — expensive and risky, but powerful if the value is
   genuinely novel and the frame makes it obvious

For solo builders at MVP stage: option 2 (niche player in existing category) is
almost always the right entry point. Create a new category only if the existing ones
actively mislead customers about your value.

## On positioning statements

A positioning statement is for internal alignment, not marketing copy.
It's a structured sentence that captures the five-component canvas in one paragraph.

Format:
> "For [best-fit customers] who [situation/pain], [product name] is a [market category]
> that [unique value]. Unlike [competitive alternatives], [product name] [key differentiator]."

Rules:
- One positioning statement, not three options
- Internal language, not headline language — it should sound analytical
- All five components present (even if compressed)
- If you can't write this without hedging, the canvas isn't done yet

## On keeping positioning loose pre-PMF

Dunford's explicit advice for early-stage: "big fish net, not tuna net."

Before product-market fit:
- You don't know which attributes resonate most
- You don't know which customer segment converts best
- You don't know which category frame reduces friction most

The right move: build a positioning thesis from your best current evidence,
state it explicitly as a hypothesis, and update it every time you learn something
from a real user who chose you (or didn't).

Signal that it's time to tighten positioning: 10+ customers who loved the product,
consistent patterns in why they chose it, and clear patterns in who didn't last.
