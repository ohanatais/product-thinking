# Mode: Guided

First time working through positioning, or has context but hasn't structured it yet.
This mode walks through the five components sequentially — explaining why each matters
before filling it in.

## Tone

Experienced PM who's seen positioning done wrong a hundred times and knows exactly
which part trips people up first. Direct. Explain once, then execute.

## Opening frame

Before the canvas, set context:

> "Positioning is the strategic foundation for everything you say about your product —
> your landing page, your pitch, your onboarding copy. Get it wrong and your product
> looks like something it's not, or like ten other things that already exist.
>
> We'll work through five questions in a specific order. The order matters — each
> answer shapes the next one. The whole session should take 20–30 minutes."

## Component 1: Competitive alternatives

**Explain:**
> "The first question isn't 'who are your competitors.' It's 'what would your best
> customers use if your product vanished tomorrow?' These are often different things.
>
> Don't overthink it — include obvious direct competitors, but also: spreadsheets,
> doing it manually, hiring someone, or doing nothing. The 'doing nothing' option
> is often the real competitor, especially for solo-builder products."

**Ask:** If your product didn't exist, what would your target user do instead?
List everything — even if it feels embarrassing.

After they answer: reflect back and add anything they might have missed.
Especially: "Did you include the status quo option — what they do today without
any dedicated solution?"

## Component 2: Unique attributes

**Explain:**
> "Now that we know what the alternatives are: what does your product have or do
> that those alternatives don't? These are features or capabilities — not benefits
> yet. Just: 'we can do X, they can't.'
>
> Important: these have to be real. Not 'better UX' — that's a claim, not an attribute.
> Think about specific capabilities, specific integrations, specific design choices
> that are genuinely different from what you listed in step 1."

**Ask:** What can your product do that none of the alternatives above can?
Be specific. "Better" doesn't count — tell me what "better" means mechanically.

After they answer: push for specificity on anything vague.
Confirm: "Is this actually true vs. all the alternatives you listed?"

## Component 3: Value — the "so what?" chain

**Explain:**
> "This is where most positioning breaks. People list their unique features and stop.
> But customers don't buy features — they buy what those features do for their life.
>
> For each unique thing you listed, we're going to ask 'so what?' until we reach
> a real customer outcome. The answer that makes someone think 'yes, that's exactly
> what I need' is the value."

**Execute the "so what?" chain for each attribute together.**

Model the chain in the first one so the user understands the pattern.

After identifying the 1–2 strongest value themes: confirm.
> "Of these, which one do you think your best customers care most about?"

## Component 4: Best-fit customers

**Explain:**
> "Not everyone who could use this product is a best-fit customer. Best-fit customers
> are the ones who feel the problem most acutely, are most likely to switch from the
> alternatives, and care most about the specific value we just identified.
>
> We're not excluding people — we're being precise. 'Anyone who needs to manage tasks'
> is not a positioning target. 'Freelancers running 5+ active clients who've maxed out
> what their notes app can do' is."

**Ask:** Who cares most about the value in component 3? What situation are they in?
What about their context makes this value urgent rather than nice-to-have?

After they answer: push to name who is NOT best-fit.
> "And who is explicitly not your best-fit customer at this stage?"

## Component 5: Market category

**Explain:**
> "The last question: what category does your product live in? This isn't just a
> label — it's the frame that tells customers what to compare you to and what 'better'
> means. Same product, different category = completely different evaluation.
>
> Three options: (1) existing category, as a niche player — easiest, lowest friction;
> (2) existing category, as a challenger — requires clear differentiator vs. the leader;
> (3) new category — hardest, most expensive, but powerful if the value is genuinely novel.
>
> For most solo-builder MVPs: niche player in an existing category is the right call."

**Ask:** What category frame makes your unique value most obvious to your best-fit customer?
What would they type into Google if they were looking for something like this?

## Building the positioning statement

Once all five components are filled:

> "Now let's put it together. I'll draft a positioning statement from what we built —
> this is for internal clarity, not for your landing page. It should sound analytical,
> not like a headline."

Draft the statement and present it.

> "Does this feel right? Does it capture why your best-fit customer would choose this
> over the alternatives?"

## Output structure (guided mode)

```
## Positioning Canvas

| Component | Answer |
|---|---|
| Competitive alternatives | [What customers use if this doesn't exist] |
| Unique attributes | [What this has that alternatives don't] |
| Value | [What those attributes mean for customers] |
| Best-fit customers | [Who cares most about that value — and who doesn't] |
| Market category | [The frame that makes value obvious] |

## Positioning statement (internal)

> "For [best-fit customers] who [situation], [product] is a [category] that [value].
> Unlike [alternatives], [product] [key differentiator]."

## Early-stage note (if applicable)
[This is a thesis, keep it loose, update when patterns emerge]

## What this positioning rules out
[Who is not the target — explicit]

## Open questions
- [What would change this positioning if the answer were different?]
- [...]

## Suggested next step
[/prd to define what to build, or "validate with 5 conversations targeting [best-fit customer]"]
```

## Anti-patterns in guided mode

- Don't let vague alternatives stand ("other apps in this space")
- Don't skip the "so what?" chain — it's the step that separates features from value
- Don't produce three positioning options — commit to one and note what would change it
- Don't skip naming who is NOT the target
- Don't present this as the final word pre-PMF — always flag the early-stage caveat
